The Idea

Each player secretly picks a combination of numbers from a shared list, and announces only its sum. From there, the chase: questions, note-taking, calculated guesses — until someone names a rival's exact combination and eliminates them. The last player standing wins.

The game starts in near-total fog and crystallises gradually into pure logic. Each player "enters the cracking phase" at a different moment — depending on how quickly they sum in their head and recognise decompositions. Mental arithmetic is the actual skill here, not homework.

Setup

  1. Agree on a number list (three calibrated lists follow) and on N — the maximum combination size.
  2. Each player secretly picks a combination of up to N numbers and writes it down. A value that appears twice in the list — both copies may be taken.
  3. Each player announces only the sum of their combination. The combination's size stays secret.
Margin note Picking your combination is the first strategic move. A sum with many decompositions protects you; an extreme sum — very low or very high — exposes you instantly. And yes: pencil and paper are mandatory. Nobody will keep track for you.

Gameplay

On their turn, a player does one of two things — ask a question, or declare an accusation.

Question

The asker addresses one other player and asks about one or more values. The respondent answers "yes" only if all the asked values are in their combination, and "no" if at least one is missing.

Dana → Yoav: "Do you have 7?" — Yes (7 is in Yoav's combo, confirmed)
Dana → Yoav: "Do you have both 4 and 9?" — No (at least one is missing. Which? Who knows)

And here's the trap: a "no" to a multi-value question doesn't reveal which value is missing. Asking about several values at once is a gamble — if you guessed right, you saved turns; if not, you got murky information you'll have to unpack with more questions.

  1. For a value that appears twice in the list, answer "yes" if at least one copy is held. The quantity stays secret.
  2. You may not repeat an identical question (same respondent, same values) that has already been asked this game.
  3. All questions and answers are spoken aloud, audible to everyone. One player's information is everyone's information — for whoever is listening and writing it down.

Accusation

A player convinced they've cracked an opponent declares that opponent's full and exact combination, including duplicates: "You have 3, 5, 5 and 8". Correct — the opponent is eliminated and the combination is revealed to all. Wrong — the accuser is eliminated instead. The wager is symmetric: accuse only when you're sure, or when you have no choice.

Automatic-reveal rule If a "yes" answer covers values whose sum exactly equals the respondent's announced sum — the combination is thereby fully revealed, and the respondent is eliminated. Such a question is effectively a safe accusation.

Victory

The last player not eliminated wins. If you stop without a clear winner — the player who confirmed the most values across their opponents (via "yes" answers) takes it.

Difficulty Knobs

The same rules serve a children's game and a forehead-furrowing one. What changes: list size, number density, duplicate count, and N. Want more? Add a turn timer — mental speed shifts from soft to hard requirement. For younger groups you can ease in: announce the combination's size too, not just the sum.

One Final Strategic Hint

Two truths you'll discover yourself, but no point waiting:

  1. Values larger than the announced sum are eliminated immediately — free deduction, before the first question.
  2. A "failed" multi-value question is a dormant asset: once you confirm some of those values later, you'll automatically know something about the rest — without asking.

Good luck. And write everything down.
